Infrastructure Costs
1.	Continuous monitoring infrastructure (Tier 3 day‑zero offering)
•	Direction: Increase (short‑term), possible decrease (long‑term)
•	Why: Moving from project‑based, one‑off audits to continuous monitoring requires always‑on telemetry collection, persistent data stores, and real‑time alerting. Initial costs: additional compute, logging, metrics pipelines, and increased storage (hot storage for recent telemetry). Over time, standardized pipelines and shared instrumentation can amortize costs and reduce per‑client marginal expense vs repeated one‑off audits.
1.	Multi‑stage analytical stack compute (Stages A–D: static forensics → consensus fusion)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Bounded symbolic proving, Monte‑Carlo adversarial simulation, multi‑agent execution and consensus fusion are compute and memory intensive. Expect higher costs for CPU/GPU instances, large memory VMs, container orchestration (Kubernetes), and possibly specialized VMs for symbolic solvers. If parallelization is used to shorten turnaround, peak compute costs will rise.
1.	Data storage and retention (evidence routing, on‑chain attestations)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Storing forensic outputs, simulation traces, cryptographic proofs, and evidence routing payloads requires durable storage and backups. On‑chain proofs reduce reliance on centralized logs but increase need for immutable off‑chain archival (for reconstruction and legal discovery). Storage class optimization (tiered hot/cold) can mitigate ongoing costs.
1.	Blockchain transaction costs (on‑chain attestation and cryptographic proofs)
•	Direction: Increase (per attestation), potential decrease in legal/insurance costs
•	Why: Writing attestations and proofs on chain incurs gas/transaction fees; frequency of attestations directly affects operating expense. Using layer‑2 or batching strategies can reduce per‑proof cost but may add complexity. However, these costs may be offset indirectly by lower liability/insurance premiums.
1.	Secure NDA / data isolation and client sandboxing
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Providing Tier 3 forensic reports under NDA requires secure multi‑tenant isolation, encrypted storage, strict access controls (IAM), audit logging, and possibly dedicated environments for sensitive clients. This increases costs for identity management, secrets management, and possibly dedicated infrastructure or VPCs.
1.	Telemetry ingestion, ETL and real‑time pipelines
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Live telemetry gating and decision‑support require streaming infrastructure (Kafka, Kinesis, Pulsar), stream processors, and ETL jobs. Running these reliably at scale adds compute and operational complexity.
1.	Agent orchestration and management (multi‑agent simulations)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Managing many specialized agents (scheduling, coordination, sandboxing, inter‑agent comms) needs orchestration layers, service discovery, and resilient job queues — increasing orchestration and monitoring costs.
1.	Testing and simulation environments (staging, fuzzing farms)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: To deliver credible Monte Carlo adversarial simulation and reduce false positives you need large, repeatable test harnesses and fuzzing farms that may run extensive stochastic campaigns. This raises costs for ephemeral compute and test data management.
1.	CI/CD and release complexity (dual‑track roadmap)
•	Direction: Increase (org overhead); neutral to slight decrease (engineering efficiency)
•	Why: Splitting product into immediate revenue track and long‑term organ upgrades increases release management complexity (parallel branches, feature flags, separate deployment pipelines), causing tool and personnel costs. However, once standardized, a clearer separation can improve engineering efficiency and reduce rework.
1.	Logging, monitoring and observability (SLAs for client assurance)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: To sell as a liability‑reducing product you must provide high‑quality, tamper‑evident logs and monitoring dashboards with SLAs (uptime, alert latency). This increases costs for observability stacks (Prometheus, Grafana, ELK/Opensearch) and retention policies.
1.	Cryptographic proof generation and verification (off‑chain and on‑chain)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: Generating verifiable, cryptographic proofs (signatures, Merkle trees, zero‑knowledge artifacts if used) requires CPU and possibly specialized libraries. Verification endpoints (APIs) and client‑facing proof verification UIs add runtime costs.
1.	Insurance, legal and compliance provisioning (indirect infra impact)
•	Direction: Net decrease in expected liability expense; operational increase in compliance tooling
•	Why: On‑chain attestations and verifiable evidence can reduce insurance premiums and legal risk (decreasing expected future costs). However, to use this in commercial contracts you will need compliance tooling (audit trails, certs, SOC‑style reporting) which increases short‑term infrastructure and audit costs.
1.	Client onboarding and demo environments (sales enablement)
•	Direction: Increase (sales ops infrastructure)
•	Why: Translating technical features to B2B collateral and supplying reproducible demo instances/PoCs requires sandboxed demo environments, sample datasets, and automation to spin up client demos quickly — incremental hosting costs and orchestration investment.
1.	API, SDK and integration support (customer integration layer)
•	Direction: Increase
•	Why: To make technical capabilities digestible to buyers you must provide stable APIs/SDKs, developer portals, and web UIs. Hosting developer docs, API gateways, authentication and rate limiting all add recurring costs.
1.	Cost optimization opportunities (mitigating increases)
•	Direction: Potential decrease (if implemented)
•	Why: Shared multi‑tenant pipelines, batching on‑chain attestations, using L2s or rollups for proofs, tiered storage, spot instances for heavy compute jobs, autoscaling and throttling of simulations can reduce marginal costs. Over time, standardized tooling and repeatable processes should lower per‑client cost vs bespoke audits.
Summary guidance on prioritization
•	Short term: Expect a meaningful increase in infrastructure spend to support continuous telemetry, heavy compute simulations, secure NDA environments, and on‑chain attestations.
•	Medium/long term: Many costs can be amortized; on‑chain attestations and verifiable proofs can produce commercial offsets (reduced legal/insurance risk, premium pricing). Invest early in batching, L2 strategies, storage tiering, and multi‑tenant shared pipelines to contain ongoing marginal costs.
